Title

My life was perfect, I was making $50,000 a month in cash buying marijuana in California and selling it in Florida. I lived on a beautiful farm outside of Redding California with chickens, guinea hens, a feral cat and rows of massive cannabis plants. It was my dream come true until it wasn’t. Which brings me to sitting in a small private plane with 100 pounds of weed, flying from St. George Utah to Tampa Florida.

In most ways, I thought I had it made. My life was a monthly circle of buying weed in Northern California, flying to Tampa for delivery of the precious cargo and then celebrating in Costa Rica to decompress.

The small commuter airport was closed when the pilot and I arrived at St. Petersburg after a 13 hour flight and two stops to refuel. Our options were either to sleep in the plane until the airport opened in the morning or throw 8 large, black duffel bags over a barbed wire fence. The decision was easy but convincing the pilot to help haul the luggage and jump a 10 foot fence was a different story.

In hindsight, breaking out of an airport is possibly just as bad as breaking into one, but we made it out and got to the hotel. The next few days were a tangle of deliveries and strange calls with one of my buyers. He would schedule a time to meet and then call at the last minute to say he couldn’t make it. I was renting the plane at a daily rate and paying the pilot by the hour, so the delay was not welcomed.

Maybe I was just being paranoid but strange things seemed to be happening. That same day, at least 4 or 5 cop cars were parked outside of my hotel, including a few undercover cars. I was already sweating from the Florida heat and day drinking since 9 am, but every last drop of moisture left my body at that instance. I had never felt that kind of fear in my life. My whole body began to shake along with the profuse sweating. This was it. They must have watched the cameras from the airport and now they were here to arrest me for breaking a handful of federal laws and to find out what was in those duffel bags.

I was frantically looking through a small slit in the curtains when I began to make out what looked like two hispanic men in handcuffs sitting on a curb of the hotel. They were in the section of rooms next to mine and one of the police vehicles had been blocking my view of the men. They weren’t here for me! Seeing this gave me some hope but I needed to get back on that plane ASAP.

My plan was to leave the weed in one of the closets in my room, pay for an extra few days at the hotel and then hide a key outside for my buyer. I can get back home and he could come pick the weed up at his leisure. A few hours later, I met the pilot at the plane and we were climbing into the clouds.

We would be flying against the jetstream on the way back so we expected a longer flight, but we had another problem. The electronics in the front wheel of the plane were not working, so the pilot was unable to raise the wheel during flight. That same paralyzing fear returned and my head began to spin. Now what? I had close to $100,000 in drug money on the plane and I did not want to go back.

After some serious convincing and light threats, the pilot agreed to continue on. His solution was to use the emergency hand crank to raise the wheel and then crank it back down when landing. It sounded easy enough but we did have two landings on the way back to refuel. The plan mostly worked, except that the hand crank was only able to lift the wheel about half way up, adding even more drag. Due to the jetstream and the extra drag from the wheel, two fuel stops turned into three and a 17 hour flight.

I know this sounds like a happy ending or at least not a complete disaster, but the fear that I had felt back in Tampa was nothing compared to what I was about to experience upon landing that plane.

We arrived in St. George Utah a good hour before daybreak so the small airport was mostly empty. Feeling some relief, I watched out the window as we taxied towards the hangers when I began seeing rows of headlights flowing towards the plane. They had been parked behind the hangers and it wasn’t two or three sets of headlights. It was easily a dozen if not 20 and they circled the plane.

The fear I felt that day only compounded when I was handed a 5 year sentence in federal prison. And that same fear would haunt me for the entirety of my prison sentence and long after. I was a good Mormon boy from Utah, not some “hardened criminal” but that fact doesn't matter in federal prison.

My experiences during that time crushed my soul, stripped away my love and hope in life and all of humanity. I lived on hate and managed it with alcohol, cocaine, weed, pills of every variety and just about anything else that came my way. It took me about eight years to find my path back to…life and hope.

I didn’t know it at the time, but my journey started on a paved walking path that runs through the local parks and partially borders a cemetery, and I don’t know how or when it will end. But I am no longer waiting to die, growth comes more naturally to me now and my soul has been reunited with my body.

But that's just my story…what’s yours?
